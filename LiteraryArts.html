Literary Arts

Romeo and Juliet – William Shakespeare

A tragic love story that transcends time, Romeo and Juliet explores the depths of youthful passion, the destructive power of familial hatred, and the role fate plays in shaping human lives. Shakespeare’s poetic language brings to life the enduring conflict between love and hate, illustrating how rash decisions can have irreversible consequences. The play remains a poignant commentary on the fragility of human existence and the consequences of conflict.

Pride and Prejudice – Jane Austen

Set in the social landscape of 19th-century England, Austen's classic novel critiques the constraints placed on women due to class and gender expectations. The novel highlights the protagonists' personal growth as they navigate their feelings for each other amidst a backdrop of societal pressures, personal biases, and misunderstandings. At its core, the novel is about overcoming prejudice, recognizing personal flaws, and the struggle for autonomy in a rigid society.

Moby-Dick – Herman Melville

Melville’s masterwork is not merely a story of a sea captain's obsession with a white whale, but a profound meditation on the complexities of the human spirit. The novel invites readers to reflect on the destructive power of obsession, the ambiguity of good versus evil, and mankind’s eternal struggle with nature's indifference. It is both an adventure narrative and an exploration of existential questions that challenge human perception.

The Raven – Edgar Allan Poe

This haunting poem embodies the darker side of the human psyche, exploring themes of grief, despair, and madness. As the narrator engages in a futile conversation with a mysterious raven, the poem delves into the torment of unresolved sorrow and the irrational fear of the unknown. Poe’s rhythm, repetition, and melancholic tone create a chilling atmosphere that reflects the torment of the mind spiraling into madness.

The Great Gatsby – F. Scott Fitzgerald

Set against the backdrop of the Jazz Age, Fitzgerald’s novel critiques the hollow pursuit of wealth and social status, highlighting the moral decay that lies beneath the glittering exterior of the American Dream. Through the tragic story of Jay Gatsby, the novel underscores the themes of unrequited love, class divisions, and the pursuit of an idealized vision of life that ultimately proves unattainable. The Great Gatsby continues to resonate as a cautionary tale about the dangers of excess and the illusion of happiness.

To Kill a Mockingbird – Harper Lee

A profound exploration of racial injustice and moral integrity, Lee's novel is both a coming-of-age story and a sharp critique of the societal norms of the American South during the 1930s. Through the eyes of Scout Finch, the novel illustrates the impact of prejudice on individuals and communities, while also showing how compassion, courage, and personal responsibility can challenge entrenched systems of discrimination.

1984 – George Orwell

Orwell’s dystopian masterpiece is a terrifying vision of a totalitarian future where the state controls every aspect of human life. Through the experiences of Winston Smith, the novel explores the loss of individuality in a society where surveillance, censorship, and manipulation are the tools of power. Orwell’s warning about the dangers of absolute control, the manipulation of truth, and the erosion of personal freedoms continues to be relevant in discussions of contemporary politics and civil rights.

The Divine Comedy – Dante Alighieri

Dante's epic journey through Hell, Purgatory, and Heaven serves as both an allegory for the soul’s journey toward redemption and a vivid commentary on the medieval world. Each level of the afterlife in the poem symbolizes various human vices and virtues, offering a layered critique of religious, political, and social issues of Dante’s time. The Divine Comedy remains a towering achievement in Western literature, examining the nature of sin, the possibility of redemption, and the ultimate pursuit of divine enlightenment.

One Hundred Years of Solitude – Gabriel García Márquez

This landmark work of magical realism chronicles the rise and fall of the Buendía family, whose fates are intertwined with the mythic and often surreal events that shape their lives. The novel weaves a complex narrative of love, solitude, and history’s repetitive cycles. By blending the magical with the real, García Márquez creates a powerful commentary on the isolation and inevitable decline of the family, while also reflecting on the broader Latin American experience.

Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone – J.K. Rowling

The first book in the beloved Harry Potter series introduces readers to a magical world filled with adventure, mystery, and self-discovery. As Harry learns of his true heritage and his place in the battle between good and evil, the novel explores themes of friendship, loyalty, and personal growth. At its heart, the book is a story about finding one's identity, facing one’s fears, and standing up for what is right, no matter the odds.
